chapter 01

1922 – 1956

they bought
streetcars to
replace them

National City Lines, is a holding company with one purpose: acquire electric streetcar networks in 45 American cities and dismantle them. Formed by General Motors, Standard Oil, and Firestone, they replaced rail with rubber, and eventually. replaced public with private.

By 1950, over 100 transit systems had been converted to diesel buses. These buses were considered to be slower, dirtier, less popular. This led to a decrease in ridership, making cars the best option.

  • 45 cities dismantled
  • $1 fine per conviction
  • 1949 antitrust verdict

"a fine of one dollar"
— GM's criminal antitrust penalty, 1949

chapter 02

june 29, 1956

$25 billion
41,000 miles
no vote required

Federal Aid Highway Act, was passed and signed by President Eisenhower in 1956. This act created the Interstate Highway System, known to be largest public works project in American history. It was funded by the new gas tax and sold as a national defense.

Construction destroyed over 300,000 homes with majority of the highways running through the hearts of Black and Brown communities. Cities that rejected freeways were able to maintain their waterfronts and walkability, while the rest lost their life.

  • 330k homes demolished, mainly targeting black and brown communities
  • 90% federally funded
  • $0 for transit

"the greatest public works project in history"
 — and not a single dollar for a train or public transportation

chapter 03

1950s – 1980s

the suburb
is a product.

With the introduction of the GI Bill, FHA loans, and redlining, America now required a car by design. Zoning laws mandated separation: homes here, shops there, jobs somewhere else entirely. The suburbs were an engineering decision with trillion-dollar consequences.

Developers lobbied for minimum parking requirements, cities then ordered seas of asphalt before a single tenant moved in. Every square foot of parking is a square foot that cannot be a home, a park, or a business.

  • 800M parking spaces in US
  • 3× more parking than cars
  • 30% of city land lost to parking

"we built the infrastructure, then called the dependency a choice"
— the great inversion

With the OPEC embargo and Gas lines stretching for miles, President Carter put solar panels on the White House and called the energy crisis. "the moral equivalent of war." Later, Reagan then removed the panels and the crisis ended, with lesson being forgotten.

During the 1980s, automobile lobbies fought fuel efficiency standards. Detroit kept building bigger and American stopped demanding something different.

  • 1981 solar panels removed
  • 55 mph national speed limit
  • only lasting 10 yrs

"the moral equivalent of war"
— Jimmy Carter, 1977 (Nobody listened)

chapter 05

1990s – 2010s

the lock-in
is the point.

The trap was complete by the 1990s. Homes were built mile away from jobs and jobs were built far from transit. Transit was underfunded because of low ridership. However, ridership was low because of its lack of its lack of convenience and the cycle continued.

Auto industry lobbying spent $700 million between 1998 and 2018 to fight fuel economy rules, EV mandates, and transit funding.

  • $700M auto lobby 1998–2018
  • 91% of US trips by car
  • 45% of the population with no access to transit

"we don't have a car problem. we have a city problem."
— Angie Schmitt, Right of Way
