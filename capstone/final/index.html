To the Petrodollar

Back into the US, A Cycle

Due to the agreement, oil exporters around the world collect U.S Dollars and reinvest them back to U.S economy. The U.S economy actively depends on this loop and the resale of oil. This maakes the U.S looses if the world shifts away from an oil centric world.

1922 - 1956

They bought streetcars to replace them

National City Lines, is a holding company with one purpose: acquire electric streetcar networks in 45 American cities and dismantle them. Formed by General Motors, Standard Oil, and Firestone, they replaced rail with rubber, and eventually replaced public with private.

By 1950, over 100 transit systems had been converted to diesel buses. These buses were considered to be slower, dirtier, less popular. This led to a decrease in ridership, making cars the best option.

  • 35 cities dismantled
  • $1 fine per conviction
  • 1949 antitrust verdict

June 29, 1956

$25 billion, 41,000 miles and no vote required

Federal Aid Highway Act, was passed and signed by President Eisenhower in 1956. This act created the Interstate Highway System, known to be largest public works project in American history. It was funded by the new gas tax and sold as a national defense.

Construction destroyed over 300,000 homes with majority of the highways running through the hearts of Black and Brown communities. Cities that rejected freeways were able to maintain their waterfronts and walkability, while the rest lost their life.

  • 330k homes demolished
  • 90% federally funded
  • $0 for transit

1950s – 1980s

The Suburb is a Product.

With the introduction of the GI Bill, FHA loans, and redlining, America now required a car by design. Zoning laws mandated separation: homes here, shops there, jobs somewhere else entirely. The suburbs were an engineering decision with trillion-dollar consequences.

Developers lobbied for minimum parking requirements, cities then ordered seas of asphalt before a single tenant moved in. Every square foot of parking is a square foot that cannot be a home, a park, or a business.

  • 800M parking spaces in the US
  • 3x more parking than cars
  • 30% of city land dedicated to parking

1973 - 1979

Two warning signs, both ignored

With the OPEC embargo and gas lines stretching for miles, President Carter put solar panels on the White House and called the energy crisis "the moral equivalent of war." Later, Reagan removed the panels and the crisis ended, with the lesson forgotten.

During the 1980s, automobile lobbies fought fuel efficiency standards. Detroit kept building bigger and Americans stopped demanding something different.

  • 1981 solar panels removed
  • 55 mph national speed limit
  • Only lasted 10 years

1990s – 2010s

The lock-in

The trap was complete by the 1990s. Homes were built miles away from jobs and jobs were built far from transit. Transit was underfunded because of low ridership. However, ridership was low because of its lack of convenience and the cycle continued.

Auto industry lobbying spent $700 million between 1998 and 2018 to fight fuel economy rules, EV mandates, and transit funding.

  • $700M auto lobby 1998-2018
  • 91% of US trips are by car
  • 45% of the population with limited to no access to public transit

2024

The bill becomes due

29% of our greenhouse gas emissions are solely from transportation, making it the largest source of emissions in the US. On average, the American household spends $10,000+ per year on car ownership, making it the second-largest expense.

40,000 Americans die in road fatalities per year with pedestrian death rate rising fast. Infrastructure designed to move cars kills the people who do not have one.

  • 29% of US emissions from transportation
  • $10K+ spent per household/year
  • 40,000 deaths caused by traffic per year
